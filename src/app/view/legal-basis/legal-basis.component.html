Legal Basis

Data protection and its importance

Data protection is the process of protecting data and thus the privacy of the person. Data is indispensable and is available at all the stages of the data processing: from its collection, use and transfer, to its storage and erasure. Data protection shall safeguard data from corruption, compromise, misuse or loss.

As more and more data is created, collected, processed, transferred and stored, the importance of data protection is constantly increasing.

Personal data security is of major interest for individuals, as personal data ending in the wrong hands can have severe consequences for the concerned individuals. Harmful consequences can vary from becoming victim of criminal activities such as phishing scams, identity theft, stolen banking information, to having private information used with harmful intent without the concerned individual’s consent.

The GDPR and European data protection regulations

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is the new European law on data protection. The GDPR entered into force on the 24 th of May 2016 and has been applicable as of the 25 th of May 2018. Since then, companies and organizations are required to be compliant with this regulation.

This regulation sets out data protection rules: it is designed to give individuals more control over their data, over how their data is collected, used and protected. It also obliges organizations and companies to comply with strict new rules on how to use and secure the personal data they collect. Ensuring transparency as well as providing individuals information on how their data is being used are the goals of this new regulation. Organizations and companies that fail to comply with these new rules face heavy penalties.

The GDPR establishes a uniform framework for data protection legislation across the EU. Instead of each member state having their own data protection laws, all member states are now governed by a single regulation. This is of great advantage for organizations and companies as they do not need to comply with 28 different data protection laws anymore, in order to offer their services in all EU member states. If this is mainly true, member states keep however the right to regulate some fields more extensively when allowed by the GDPR. Hence slight regulatory differences between member states might remain.

In the context of this project, legal questions on data protection raised by the creation of a health related app will only be discussed with regards to the GDPR. National regulations will not be taken into account.

Concerning the material scope of the GDPR, the regulation applies to the processing of personal data, when this data is entirely or partially processed by automated systems or when it is processed with the intention to integrate this data in a filing system (art. 2 GDPR). In the light of the above statement, if no personal data is processed (explanations on what “personal data” and “process” mean will be given in the section “some definitions”), the GDPR doesn’t apply. Furthermore, there are a few exceptions where the GDPR doesn’t apply when personal data is processed (cf. art. 2(2) GDPR). One of these exceptions is the processing of personal data in the context of purely personal or household activities.

Regarding the territorial scope of the GDPR, the regulation applies to any organization established within the European Union, as well as to any other organization within or outside the EU but that processes personal data of EU residents (art. 3 GDPR). This is the so- called “extra-territorial effect” of the GDPR.

As the health related mobile application discussed in this project should process personal data of EU residents with an automated system, the GDPR applies to it. Hence, our company has to make sure to comply with the GDPR rules if it does not want to face heavy penalties, bad branding image and the loss of the users’ trust.

Definitions
Personal data (Art. 4(1) GDPR)

Personal data is any information that relates to a living individual who can be directly or indirectly identified.

This excludes information related to companies from the definition of personal data. Therefore data concerning companies doesn’t fall within the scope of the GDPR.

For information to be considered personal data, the format isn’t relevant. Personal data can be contained on video, audio, numerical, graphical, photographic supports etc.

Individuals are directly identifiable if you can identify them by exclusively using information you already possess.

Individuals are indirectly identifiable if you can not identify them exclusively using information you already possess, but who might be identified by using other information, information you can reasonably get access to through external sources or third parties.

Personal data that has been pseudonymised or encrypted can be processed to re-identify individuals. This kind of data remains personal data and therefore falls within the scope of the GDPR.

Personal data that has been irreversibly anonymized is no longer considered personal data and therefore doesn’t fall within the scope of the GDPR. However, companies must be very careful in this context, as it is rare and extremely difficult to successfully anonimyze data irreversibly.

Here is a non-exhaustive list of information possibly falling under the definition of personal data: name, email address, location information, ethnicity, gender, biometric data, religious beliefs, web cookies, and political opinions etc.

Personal data (Art. 4(1) GDPR)

Data concerning health is personal data related to the physical or mental health of a living individual, including the provision of health care services, which reveal information about his or her health status.

Sensitive (personal) data

Sensitive data is a special category of personal data. To ensure a higher level of protection, this category of data is subject to stricter rules than regular personal data.

Genetic and biometric data, as well as data on racial or ethnic origin, on political opinions, on religious or philosophical beliefs, on trade union membership, concerning health, concerning sex life or concerning sexual orientation are considered sensitive personal data.

They must be processed with extra security, as the loss or misuse of this category of data would be particularly prejudicial for the concerned individuals and could involve severe and unacceptable risks for fundamental human rights and freedoms.

Data subject

The data subject is the (directly or indirectly) identifiable living individual the data is about and whose data is processed.

Data processing (Art. 4(2) GDPR)

Any action performed on personal data is data processing. It can by manual or automated. Collection, recording, organization, structuring, storage, adaptation or alteration, retrieval, consultation, use, disclosure by transmission, dissemination or otherwise making available, alignment or combination, restriction, erasure or destruction of data is data processing.

Data controller (Art. 4(7) GDPR)

The data controller is the individual or organization which decides if and how personal data will be processed.

The data controller is the individual or organization which decides if and how personal data will be processed.

Data processor (Art. 4(8) GDPR)

The data processor is a third party individual or organization which processes personal data on behalf of the data controller.

Pseudonymisation (Art. 4(5) GDPR)

Pseudonymisation is the processing of personal data in such a way that the personal data can no longer be attributed to a specific data subject without the use of additional information. In addition, pseudonymisation requires for such additional information to be kept separately and to be subject to technical and organizational measures to ensure that the personal data is not attributed to an identified or identifiable individual.

Third party (Art. 4(10) GDPR)

A third party is any individual or organization other than the data subject, controller, processor who, with the permission of the data controller or processor, is authorized to process personal data.

Observed Principles when processing Your Data

Article 5 GDPR lists different principles to be observed by the data controller (us, our company) when processing personal data (your personal data). Here are those principles:

LAWFULNESS, FAIRNESS AND TRANSPARENCY (Art. 5(1)(a) GDPR)

The data controller must comply with the law. Data has to be collected and processes under valid grounds (“lawful reasons for data processing”) as per the GDPR. In addition, the data processing must be fair and transparent to the data subject, in particular through the establishment of a privacy policy. This means the data controller must not process the data in a way that is unduly detrimental, unexpected or misleading to the data subject. The data controller must be clear, open and honest with the data subject from the start about how they will use collected data.

Our Compliance

To comply with this principle our company has identified a lawful basis (consent) for the processing of personal data and does not do anything unlawful with it. Our company uses a privacy policy and does not deceive or mislead individuals when collecting their personal data. Our company is transparent and honest and complies with the right to be informed.
PURPOSE LIMITATION (Art. 5(1)(b) GDPR)

The data controller must process data for the legitimate purposes specified explicitly to the data subject when the data was collected.

Our Compliance

Our company complies with this principle by clearly identifying the different purposes for our processing of personal data. Those purposes are documented and detailed in the app’s privacy policy. If our company needs to use personal data for new purposes that are not compatible with the ones mentioned in the policy at the time consent was given, our company will ask for the user’s renewed consent before processing any additional personal data in the context of the new purposes.
DATA MINIMIZATION (Art. 5(1)(c) GDPR)

The data controller should collect and process solely adequate and relevant data, and only as much of it as absolutely necessary for the specified purposes.

Our Compliance

To comply with this principle our company only collects personal data we actually need for the specified purposes. Our company periodically reviews the personal data it holds and anything irrelevant is deleted from our database.
ACCURACY (Art. 5(1)(d) GDPR)

Accuracy of the collected personal data is part of the principles of data protection. The data controller must maintain personal data accurate and up to date. The data controller must take reasonable measures to make sure the personal data he holds is not misleading or incorrect. If the data controller does discover that pieces of personal data are incorrect, he must correct or erase them.

Our Compliance

To comply with this principle our company ensures the accuracy of any personal data we process by having appropriate procedures in place. In this context, our company complies with the individual’s right to rectification.
STORAGE LIMITATION (Art. 5(1)(e) GDPR)

Following the same logic as for the principle of data minimization, the data controller should store personal data only for as long as necessary for the specified purposes.

Our Compliance

To comply with this principle our company keeps record of which personal data it holds and why it remains important to keep it. Our company can justify why and how long personal data is kept. In this context, our company ensures individuals’ right to request the erasure of their data is respected.
INTEGRITY AND CONFIDENTIALITY (Art. 5(1)(f) GDPR)

The data controller must ensure appropriate security, integrity and confidentiality of the data processing.

Our Compliance

You will find more information on how our company complies with this principle in the “Security of Your Data” section.
ACCOUNTABILITY (Art. 5(2) GDPR)

The data controller must be able to demonstrate GDPR compliance with all of these principles. Not being able to demonstrate the company’s compliance with the GDPR amounts to not complying with the regulation.

Our Compliance

Our company complies with this principle by keeping evidence of the steps taken to abide by the GDPR. It keeps documentation regarding its compliance available at any time to the app users.

Each of these principles is developed throughout the GDPR and concretized by specific and detailed articles of the regulation.

Your Rights over Your Data

The GDPR recognizes a series of new rights for individual’s whose personal data is collected and processed. This should enable them to have better control over their personal data, even once their data is in the hands of a data controller. Here is a list of the data subjects’ rights provided by the GDPR:

THE RIGHT TO BE INFORMED (Art. 12 GDPR)

Data subjects have the right to be informed about the collection and the processing of their data. Data subject have the right to be provided by the data controller with information such as: the type of personal data collected, the purpose of the processing, the way their data is collected, processed, stored and disclosed. Data subject have the right to concise, transparent, intelligible and easily accessible information on their data.

Our Compliance

Our company informs users on these different matters through its privacy policy and is contactable in case of any questions.
THE RIGHT OF ACCESS (Art. 15 GDPR)

Data subjects have the right to access personal data the data controller holds about them, free of charge (as long as the request is reasonable). The data subject should get a response within a month.

Our Compliance

Our company will endeavour to respond to any user’s request within a month.
THE RIGHT TO RECTIFICATION (Art. 16 GDPR)

Data subjects have to right to have their inaccurate personal data rectified or completed. The rectification should be done within one month after their request.

Our Compliance

Our company will respond to any user’s request within a month.
THE RIGHT TO ERASURE (Art. 17 GDPR)

Data subjects have the right to have their personal data erased. This right is also known as the right to be forgotten. The data should be erased within one month after the their request. However this right is not absolute. It only applies under certain circumstances.

Our Compliance

Our company will respond to users’ legitimate requests within a month.
THE RIGHT TO RESTRICT PROCESSING (Art. 18 GDPR)

Data subjects have the right to require restrictions on the processing of their personal. If the processing is restricted, the data controller can store the data, but is not allowed to use it. The processing restriction should be implemented within one month after the request. However the right to restrict processing is not absolute. It only applies under certain circumstances.

Our Compliance

Our company will respond to users’ legitimate requests within a month.
THE RIGHT TO DATA PORTABILITY (Art. 20 GDPR)

Data subject have the right to transfer their personal data from one data controller to another, and hence obtain and reuse their personal data in their own interest with another service provider. They should be able to copy and transfer personal data easily, safely and securely, with no alteration of its usability from one data controller to another.

Our Compliance

When requested by users, our company will make sure to provide them with a support containing their full data and easy to reuse.
THE RIGHT TO OBJECT (Art. 21 GDPR)

Data subjects have the right to object to the processing of their personal data. This isn’t an absolute right and depends on the circumstances. The data controller might keep processing the data if he has a compelling reason to do so. However data subjects have an absolute right to require the cessation of the use of their personal data for direct marketing. A response to the objection should be given to the data subject within one month after its request.

Our Compliance

Our company will respond to users’ legitimate requests within a month.

Lawfulness of data processing and consent
Lawfulness of personal data processing

In accordance with Article 6 GDPR the processing of personal data is only lawful under certain circumstances. If one of the following requirements is met, the concerned personal data processing is lawful:

  1. The data subject has given consent to the processing of his or her personal data for one or more specific purposes
  2. The processing of personal data is necessary to perform a contract to which the data subject is party or in order to take steps at the request of the data subject prior to entering into a contract.
  3. The processing of personal data is necessary to comply with legal obligations from Union law or Member State law to which the data controller is subject (Art. 6(3) GDPR).
  4. The processing of personal data is necessary in order to protect the vital interests of the data subject or of another natural person
  5. The processing of personal data is necessary to perform a task in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority (in cases where the data controller has this authority).
  6. The processing of personal data is necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the data controller or by a third party, except where such interests are overridden by the interests or fundamental rights and freedoms of the data subject.
Our Compliance

In the context of the operation of our app and the resultant data processing, the most relevant lawful reasons for personal data processing would probably be: consent of the data subject, performance of a binding contract with the data subject and compliance with legal obligations. Other lawful reasons for data processing could be possible in our case, but would be less likely to occur. As long as our company legitimately relies on one of the mentioned reasons every time it processes data, then our personal data processing is lawful.

For our company to rely on the consent of the data subject or the existence of a contract requesting data processing for its performance, we will need to get that consent from our users or to have a contractual relation with them, probably based on a service agreement. As obtaining our users’ consent is the safest option in our opinion, this is probably what our company will go for.

Under the GDPR the process of obtaining consent is subject to specific rules, which will be discussed at the end of this section.
Lawfulness of sensitive data processing

As the processing of sensitive personal data requires a higher level of protection than the processing of personal data due to the inherent risk of this processing, special rules apply. According to Article 9 GDPR processing sensitive personal data is basically forbidden. However, there are exceptions and the prohibition can be lifted if one of the requirements listed in Article 9(2) GDPR is met.

Hence, for the lawful processing of sensitive personal data, the data controller is required to have a valid exception motif based on Article 9 GDPR, in addition to a lawful reason to process personal data based on Article 6 GDPR.

Our Compliance

We will not mention all the possible exemption cases from Article 9(2) GDPR as there are many and only one of them would apply in the case of our app: again, it will be obtaining consent from our users for the processing of their personal data, and in this case for the processing of their sensitive personal data.
Obtaining consent under the GPPR

The GDPR sets a high standard for the obtention of consent. The conditions for consent are defined in Article 7 GDPR. Additionally, Article 8 GDPR defines the applicable conditions for obtaining a child’s consent.

Consent must be freely given, specific, informed and unambiguous.

“Freely given” consent essentially means that the data controllers should not corner the data subject into agreeing to the processing of their data. Hence, the obtention of the consent cannot be presented as a condition for using the company services. The data subject must have genuine and free choice and must be able to refuse or withdraw consent without detriment.

“Specific consent” means that the request for consent should be presented in a way that clearly distinguishes this question from other matters. The data controller must get separate consent for each type of data processing operation. He must give the user an opportunity to confirm or decline each type of use. If the data controller has more than one purpose to conduct data processing, he must obtain consent for each different purpose.

“Informed consent” means the data subject knows the data processor’s identity, which personal data will be processed, the purpose of the data processing, and users must be made aware of their right to withdraw their consent at any time. It also means that these pieces of information have to be shared in an intelligible and easily accessible form, using clear and plain language.

“Unambiguous consent” means it should be a certainty that the data subject has consented. The data controller must provide an opt-in system for the collection of the data subject’s consent. Silence, pre-ticked boxes or inactivity does not constitute consent.

Consent can be revoke. Data subjects have the right to withdraw their consent at any time. The data controller must make the consent withdrawal process uncomplicated and easily accessible.

In the context of the processing of sensitive personal data based on the data subject’s consent, the process to obtain consent is even more rigorous. Additional information from the ones mentioned above might need to be provided and the data processor must explain even more clearly how the data will be processed.

Our Compliance

Our company will comply with all these requirements through its privacy policy. All provided information will be clear and easily understandable by everyone. The app users will be able to give their consent regarding the processing of their personal data, as well as over the processing of their sensitive data. The users will opt-in if they consent, and will be able to do so for each different processing activity on their data. Our company will inform them about their right to withdrawal and will make sure to obtain from its users freely given, specific, informed and unambiguous consent only.

Regarding children’s consent, our company will not collect or process any data from individuals under the age of 16. Therefore it doesn’t have to comply with any additional rules.

Security of personal data and compliance tools

The GDPR imposes many different legal obligations on the data controller in order to make sure personal data is well safeguarded. The regulation also imposes obligations to ensure the right measures are taken in case of a data breach. The data processor has legal obligations as well, but this doesn’t concern our company, as we don’t rely on processors. In accordance with the accountability principle (cf. section “Observed Principle when processing Your Data”), the data controller is responsible for complying with the Regulation and needs to establish appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure data protection.

Here is an overview of some of these legal tools and obligations, classified in three different categories: the tools and obligations to prevent any security issue, the tools and obligations regarding the security methods themselves and the obligations in case of a data breach.

Preventive security tools and obligations
Data protection by design and by default (Art. 25 GDPR)

Data protection by design means that the data controller needs to consider and take into account data protection and privacy issues in everything the company does, at the earliest stages of processing.

Data protection by default means the data controller has to ensure he processes necessary personal data only to achieve the specified purpose (cf. principle of minimization in the section “Observed Principle when processing Your Data”). The data controller has to adopt a privacy-first approach. For example, the data controller should ensure that personal data is not automatically made public unless users decide to change the settings. By default the settings should protect the user privacy.

Our Compliance

Our company had a safety-first approach from the start while developing the app. Everything has been done so that the users’ personal data is well protected. The settings by default of the app are programmed to allow minimum data processing.
Data Protection impact assessment (Art. 35 GDPR)

A Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) is a process to help the data controller identify the risk inherent to his processing of personal data in the context of his company’s activities. Hence the goal of the DPIA is to minimize risks to the rights and freedoms of data subjects. The data controllers must do such an assessment when the data processing process is considered high risk for the users, as it is for instance the case when sensitive personal data is processed.

Our Compliance

As our company processes sensitive personal data on a large scale, a DPIA was conducted. It resulted positively, ensuring all appropriate measures are taken to protect personal data the company processes.
Prior consultation (Art. 36 GDPR)

When the DPIA indicates that the processing of personal would be of high risk and further measures have not been taken, the data controller must consult the competent supervisory authority before beginning data processing. This authority can then, if necessary, provide advice to the company.

Our Compliance

This step has not been necessary for our company.
Data protection officer (Art 37-39 GDPR)

The data controller has to designate a data protection officer among others when the main activities of the data controller consist on processing on a large scale special data, such as sensitive personal data. The data protection officer should be expert in data protection laws and practices. His tasks are to inform and advise the data controllers and the company’s employees about data protection requirements, to monitor the company’s GDPR compliance, to cooperate with the supervisory authority responsible for data protection, to act as contact point with the data protection authorities, etc.

Our Compliance

Our company has a data protection officer to monitor and control our compliance with the GDPR at all times.
Records of processing activities (Art. 30 GDPR)

The data controller has to maintain a record of processing activities under its responsibility, referencing: the company’s name and contact details, the purpose of processing, a description of the processed data, the recipients to whom data has been or will be disclosed, transfers of data, the envisaged time limit for erasure, description of the data security measures, etc. This record must be made available to the supervisory authority.

Our Compliance

Keeping such a record is not mandatory for every company. As our company is processing sensitive personal data this obligation applies to us. Hence, our company keeps a record of our processing activities.
Obligations regarding securing methods
Security of processing (Art. 32 GDPR)

Taking multiples factors into account (the costs of implementation, the context and purposes of processing, the probable risk presented by the processing for the rights and freedoms of the data subjects, etc.), the data controller must implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk. Amongst others, Pseudonymisation and Encryption can be appropriate methods.

Our Compliance

Our company has organizational as well as technical appropriate measures to ensure the security of the users’ data. Our company uses pseudonymisation as well as encryption methods to achieve that.
Obligation in case of a data breach
Notification of a personal data breach to the supervisory authority (Art. 33 GDPR)

In the case of a personal data breach, the data controller must notify it to the competent supervisory authority without undue delay and not later than 72 hours after having become aware of it (when feasible). This aims at minimizing adverse effects.

Our Compliance

In case of a data breach, our company can abide by the law and will notify the competent authority.
Communication of a personal data breach to the data subject (Art. 34 GDPR)

If the personal data breach is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of the data subject, the data controller must communicate the personal data breach to her/him without undue delay. This communication must describe in clear and plain language the nature of the personal data breach.

International transfers of data

The GDPR primarily applies to data controllers located in EU/EEA member states. Hence, when personal data is transferred outside of these territories, there is a risk for data subjects to lose the protection of the GDPR. In the light of the above considerations, the GDPR provides special rules on international personal data transfers (Art. 44-50 GDPR).

In accordance with Article 44 GDPR, personal data can only be transferred to third countries when certain conditions regarding data security are met in the third country where data is transferred, as well as in other third countries where the data could further be transferred.

The data controller can lawfully transfer personal data on the basis of an “adequacy decision” (Art. 45 GDPR). Such a decision is taken by the European Commission, when it has decided that a third country, a territory or specified sectors within a third country ensure an adequate level of protection.

In the absence of an adequacy decision the data controller can transfer personal data to a third country or an international organization only if the controller has put in place appropriate safeguards for the security of the data and on condition that legal remedies are available to data subjects (Art. 46 GDPR).

If the international transfer does not fulfil the conditions of Article 45 GDPR, nor the ones in Article 46 GDPR, then the transfer is most likely not lawful. There are however some exceptions (Art. 49 GDPR), one of them being the data subject’s consent. Agreements between countries regarding the implementation of appropriate safeguards are relevant as well and must be taken into account.
